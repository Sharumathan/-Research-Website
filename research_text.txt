V oice Agentic Smart Reader Application In Sinhala
and Tamil For Visually Impaired People
Abstract—Access to documents, news articles, and printed
content in low-resource languages such as Sinhala and Tamil
remains a significant challenge for visually impaired and reading-
challenged users. While recent advances in computer vision,
speech processing, and large language models (LLMs) have
enabled powerful document understanding capabilities, existing
systems often operate in isolation, providing limited support for
non-visual navigation, emotional expressiveness, and interactive
knowledge access. This research presents a multi-component
intelligent smart reading system that integrates document ac-
quisition, structured understanding, interactive retrieval, and
emotionally expressive speech synthesis for Sinhala and Tamil
languages. The proposed system consists of four tightly cou-
pled modules. First, an Intelligent Voice-Guided Navigation
(IVGN) component enables non-visual document capture by
transforming real-time geometric information from a seman-
tic segmentation backbone into adaptive, multilingual auditory
guidance, ensuring stable alignment and reliable auto-capture
of OCR-ready images. Second, a Tamil article understanding
and question answering module performs semantic paragraph
clustering and safe heading generation, followed by an article-
grounded Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline with
controlled hybrid web fallback to support accurate, context-
aware querying. Additionally, the module tracks user reading
behavior to recommend related news articles retrieved from
the internet and uses reinforcement learning to continuously
improve personalization. Third, a voice-assisted Sinhala smart
reader enables non-linear document navigation through Sinhala
voice command classification, semantic segmentation, and vector-
based retrieval, allowing users to interact with documents using
natural spoken instructions. Finally, an Emotional Sinhala and
Tamil Text-to-Speech (TTS) component generates emotionally
expressive speech using emotion-conditioned acoustic modeling
and controllable emotion intensity, enhancing the naturalness and
communicative effectiveness of auditory output under limited
data conditions. Experimental evaluations across components
demonstrate reliable document capture, effective topic segmenta-
tion, accurate document-grounded question answering, improved
navigation efficiency, and emotionally distinguishable speech
synthesis. Together, these contributions form a unified, accessible,
and human-centric smart reading framework that advances
intelligent document interaction for underrepresented languages.

I. INTRODUCTION
Access to textual information is essential for education,
employment, and independent living. However, visually im-
paired and reading-challenged users continue to face sig-
nificant barriers when inter- acting with printed and digital
documents, particularly in low-resource language contexts
such as Sinhala and Tamil. Although advances in computer
vision, speech processing, and natural language understanding
have enabled powerful assistive technologies, existing systems
often address individual sub-tasks in isolation, resulting in
fragmented user experiences that lack seamless interaction,
adaptability, and accessibility. Mobile document acquisition
systems have substantially improved access to printed content
through advances in optical character recognition (OCR) [1],
[4]. Nevertheless, accurate OCR performance remains highly
dependent on proper geometric alignment, scale consistency,
and illumination during image capture. For visually impaired
users, achieving such alignment without visual feedback is a
major challenge. Most existing document scanning applica-
tions rely on visual overlays or static audio prompts, which
provide limited contextual awareness and fail to adapt to docu-
ment orientation or user behavior. As a result, users experience
repeated corrections, prolonged capture times, and reduced
usability during document acquisition. Beyond capture, in-
teracting with extracted text presents additional challenges.
Conventional screen readers typically operate using linear
reading paradigms, forcing users to listen sequentially rather
than allowing efficient navigation based on intent. This lim-
itation is particularly pronounced in low-resource languages
such as Sinhala, where intelligent voice-driven document nav-
igation systems remain underdeveloped. Sinhala is morpho-
logically rich and permits multiple linguistic variations for the
same semantic command, complicating robust voice command
recognition and intent mapping. As a result, users lack the
ability to naturally navigate documents, request summaries,
or access specific content sections through spoken interaction.
Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have
significantly improved text understanding, summarization, and
question answering in high-resource languages [7], [11], [12],
[15]. However, similar capabilities for low-resource languages
such as Tamil remain limited, especially in structured news
article understanding and document-grounded question an-
swering. Tamil news articles often contain multiple subtopics
distributed across paragraphs without explicit structural bound-
aries, making topic-aware summarization and focused query-
ing difficult. Direct application of LLM-based generation
without grounding further risks hallucinated or unreliable re-
sponses. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) frameworks
mitigate this issue by grounding generation in retrieved con-
text [8], [19], yet their application to structured Tamil article
understanding and adaptive retrieval remains underexplored.
In addition to navigation and understanding, speech output
quality plays a critical role in assistive systems. Text-to-Speech
(TTS) technology serves as the primary interface through
which visually impaired users access documents, summaries,
and interactive responses. While modern neural TTS systems
achieve high intelligibility in high-resource languages, TTS
solutions for Sinhala and Tamil largely produce neutral and
monotonous speech. The lack of emotional expressiveness
reduces user engagement, comprehension, and the perceived
naturalness of interaction, particularly in long-form reading
and assistive feedback scenarios. Emotional speech synthesis
in low-resource languages is further constrained by limited
datasets, speaker imbalance, and recording artifacts. To address
these challenges holistically, this research presents a multi-
component intelligent smart reading system designed for low-
resource languages. The system integrates four complemen-
tary components: (1) an Intelligent V oice-Guided Navigation
(IVGN) module that enables nonvisual document capture by
transforming real-time geometric information from semantic
segmentation into adaptive, multilingual auditory guidance; (2)
a voice-assisted Sinhala smart reader that supports non-linear
document navigation through natural Sinhala voice command
classification, semantic segmentation, and retrieval-augmented
summarization; (3) a Tamil article understanding and question
answering module that performs semantic topic segmentation,
safe heading generation, and document-grounded RAG with
controlled hybrid web fallback; and (4) an Emotional Sinhala
and Tamil Text-to-Speech component that generates emo-
tionally expressive speech using emotion-conditioned acoustic
modeling with controllable intensity under limited data condi-
tions. By unifying intelligent document acquisition, structured
understanding, interactive retrieval, and emotionally expressive
speech synthesis, the proposed system advances toward a
more accessible, adaptive, and human-centric smart reading
framework. This work contributes practical solutions for in-
telligent document interaction in underrepresented language
environments and demonstrates the feasibility of building
end-to-end assistive reading systems tailored to the needs of
visually impaired users.
II. RELATEDWORK
Prior work relevant to this study spans OCR-enabled doc-
ument acquisition, non-visual guidance, multilingual NLP,
retrieval-augmented generation, and emotional speech synthe-
sis. Transformer-based OCR and document quality assessment
methods have improved text extraction robustness, while as-
sistive guidance studies show that adaptive auditory feedback
can significantly reduce user burden during capture. In low-
resource settings, recent multilingual transformer models and
parameter-efficient adaptation methods have improved classi-
fication, summarization, and question-answering performance,
but robust Sinhala/Tamil integration remains limited. Existing
screen-reader workflows are still largely linear and neutral in
speech delivery, highlighting the need for a unified system that
combines non-visual capture, structured retrieval, voice-based
navigation, and expressive TTS in one end-to-end architecture.
III. METHODOLOGY
The proposed system is implemented as a modular pipeline
with four coordinated components: (i) Intelligent V oice-
Guided Navigation (IVGN) for non-visual capture, (ii) Tamil
article understanding with retrieval-augmented question an-
swering, (iii) Sinhala voice-command navigation for non-
linear reading, and (iv) emotion-aware Sinhala/Tamil TTS. The
pipeline starts from mobile document capture, applies OCR
and language routing, and then activates language-specific
modules before producing expressive speech output.
A. System Architecture Overview
The overall architecture follows a modular and language-
aware design that supports document capture, OCR, seman-
tic understanding, question answering, voice navigation, and
expressive speech output within a single assistive workflow.
IVGN estimates document geometry in real time and converts
alignment errors into prioritized auditory cues, while automatic
capture is triggered only after stable geometric consistency is
observed across consecutive frames. After OCR and language
identification, Tamil content is segmented into semantically
coherent blocks and processed through grounded retrieval and
controlled fallback for question answering. Sinhala content
is routed to a voice-command navigation layer that com-
bines intent classification, state-aware command handling,
summarization, and targeted retrieval. The final responses are
delivered through emotion-aware TTS, enabling accessible and
natural voice interaction across the end-to-end pipeline.
B. Datasets
This research adopts a comprehensive and well-structured
dataset strategy to address emotion detection, emotional
speech synthesis, document understanding, and voice-assisted
interaction for Sinhala and Tamil, both of which are low-
resource languages. Special attention is given to collecting
realistic and diverse data that reflects natural language usage,
cultural expression, and real-world operating conditions. For
emotion classification, the ACTSEA dataset is used, combin-
ing large-scale Twitter data with manual annotation across six
basic emotions so that the models learn informal language
patterns, slang, and morphological variation commonly seen
in social media [14].
To support emotional text-to-speech, a custom Sinhala
emotional speech dataset containing 500 audio samples was
recorded in a controlled setting, with each sentence expressed
in happiness, sadness, anger, fear, and neutral styles. Expert
review from a drama specialist was used to improve emotional
authenticity, which is important for natural and expressive
synthesis. The dataset is also referenced through the Hugging
Face release Sinhala Emotional TTS. For broader language
understanding, the system incorporates the Navanjana Sinhala
dataset together with a structured Tamil news dataset to capture
long-form discourse, semantic relations, and topic transitions
needed for summarization, segmentation, and contextual com-
prehension.

IV. RESULTS
A. Emotion Classification Results
The emotion classification component was evaluated for
both Tamil and Sinhala using transformer-based multilingual
models. Experiments were conducted with DistilBERT, Sin-
BERT, and XLM-RoBERTa architectures. Models were trained
on labeled emotion datasets and evaluated using accuracy and
macro F1-score.
As shown in Table I, XLM-RoBERTa achieved the best
performance for both languages, with 85.43% accuracy for
Tamil and 87% for Sinhala. This performance gain is attributed
to stronger multilingual contextual embeddings, which better
capture semantic relationships in morphologically rich lan-
guages.
DistilBERT achieved moderate performance for Tamil,
while SinBERT showed lower performance for Sinhala, likely
due to dataset complexity and linguistic variability in emo-
tional expression. Nevertheless, all models demonstrated the
feasibility of emotion classification in low-resource settings,
supporting integration with the emotion-aware text-to-speech
pipeline.
Tamil emotion classification achieved strong results for
Happy, Fear, and Surprise, which often exhibit clearer cues
in social-media-style text. Lower performance for Anger and
Sadness likely reflects lexical overlap among negative emotion

B. Camera-Guided Document Capture Results
To validate the proposed camera-guided document capture
module, all candidate models were trained on an NVIDIA RTX
3060 using 5-fold cross-validation and deployed on a mid-
range Android device using PyTorch Lite for edge inference.
Table IV summarizes mean Intersection over Union (mIoU),
pixel accuracy, F1 score, inference latency, and model size.
BiSeNetV2 was selected as the best edge-deployment candi-
date, achieving 2.8 ms inference while maintaining strong seg-
mentation accuracy (97.16% mIoU). Its compact size (7.6 MB)
and low memory footprint support real-time 30 FPS guidance
on resource-constrained devices, which is critical for continu-
ous on-device camera feedback without cloud dependency. The
overall comparative behavior across candidate architectures is
visualized in the Model Comparison Dashboard (Fig. 1), which
complements Table IV by providing an at-a-glance view of the
accuracy–latency trade-off.

C. Tamil Question Answering Module Results
The experiments were conducted using the base model
Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct. To adapt the model for Tamil question
answering, parameter-efficient fine-tuning was performed us-
ing the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) technique. Addition-
ally, a reinforcement-learning recommendation module based
on an epsilon-greedy contextual bandit tracks user reading
behavior and updates category preferences using feedback to
personalize news recommendations. Training and evaluation
were carried out using the CHAI Tamil Question Answering
dataset [13], which contains Tamil context passages, questions,
and corresponding answers. The dataset was divided into
training and testing subsets, and model performance was
evaluated on a held-out test set.
Performance was assessed using widely adopted natural
language generation metrics, including Accuracy, ROUGE [9],
BLEU [10], and Exact Match. Table V reports the comparative
performance across the base model, LoRA fine-tuned model,
and improved training configuration, while Fig. 2 presents a
qualitative Tamil QA output sample.
The results indicate that LoRA-based fine-tuning signifi-
cantly improves the performance of the base model for Tamil
question answering tasks. Accuracy increased from 0.69 in
the base model to 0.78 after LoRA fine-tuning, and further
improved to 0.88 with the enhanced training configuration.
ROUGE scores also increased after fine-tuning, suggesting
better lexical overlap between generated and reference an-
swers. Additionally, the BLEU score showed gradual improve-
ment, indicating stronger n-gram similarity between predicted

and ground-truth responses.
The Exact Match score remained zero, which is common
in generative question answering systems, as models tend to
produce paraphrased or explanatory answers rather than exact
string matches with the reference answers.
Overall, the results demonstrate that parameter-efficient
fine-tuning with LoRA effectively enhances the capability of
the Qwen2.5-3B model for Tamil question answering in low-
resource language settings.
D. Sinhala Voice Command Intent Classification Module Re-
sults
The Sinhala V oice Command Intent Classification Module
is responsible for interpreting user voice commands and map-
ping them to predefined document interaction intents such
as navigation, summarization, and question answering. The
module processes the transcribed speech input and identifies
the corresponding intent to determine the appropriate system
action.
To evaluate the effectiveness of this component, a Sinhala
voice command dataset was constructed and divided using an
80/20 stratified train–test split. A Linear Support Vector Ma-
chine (SVM) classifier with TF-IDF character n-gram features
was used to model the intent classification task.
The model achieved an overall accuracy of 98.39%, demon-
strating strong performance in recognizing Sinhala voice
commands across different interaction categories. Most intent
classes achieved near-perfect precision, recall, and F1 scores,
indicating reliable classification performance. As summarized
in Table VI and visualized in Fig. 3, minor misclassifications
were observed mainly between semantically similar intents
such as WHERE and ANSWER LONG.
These results demonstrate that the proposed intent classi-
fication model provides accurate and reliable interpretation

E. Comparison With Existing Systems
This subsection situates the proposed V oice Agentic Smart
Reader within the landscape of existing assistive reading
technologies. While commercial tools such as KNFB Reader,
Microsoft Seeing AI, Google Lookout, JAWS, NVDA, and
Apple V oiceOver have advanced document accessibility for
mainstream languages, they typically address isolated parts of
the reading pipeline and provide little or no support for Sinhala
and Tamil. In contrast, the proposed system offers a unified
framework for intelligent capture, non-linear voice navigation,
retrieval-augmented question answering, and emotionally ex-
pressive speech synthesis.
Document Capture and OCR:Commercial applications
such as KNFB Reader and Microsoft Seeing AI rely mainly on
cloud-based OCR and offer limited or visual-only alignment
support, while Google Lookout provides partial audio cues
but still depends on connectivity. By contrast, the proposed
IVGN module runs on-device using BiSeNetV2 (7.6 MB, 2.8
ms inference) and delivers geometry-aware audio guidance at
30 FPS without network dependency, which is important for
low-connectivity environments and Sinhala/Tamil document
capture.
Navigation Paradigm:Mainstream screen readers—
including JAWS, NVDA, and Apple V oiceOver—largely fol-
low a linear reading model based on sequential shortcuts
or gestures. The proposed system enables non-linear Sinhala
voice navigation through a Linear SVM with TF-IDF character
n-gram features, achieving 98.39% intent classification accu-
racy for commands such as section jump, summarize, repeat,
and targeted retrieval.
Question Answering and Knowledge Retrieval:None of
the surveyed commercial systems provide document-grounded
question answering. The proposed Tamil Q&A module ad-
dresses this gap through a Retrieval-Augmented Generation
pipeline built on a LoRA fine-tuned Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct
model, achieving 0.88 accuracy on the CHAI Tamil QA
benchmark, while controlled hybrid fallback handles out-of-
document queries and reduces hallucination risk.
Text-to-Speech Expressiveness:Existing screen-reader
TTS engines generally produce neutral speech with no affec-
tive control. The proposed Emotional TTS module introduces
emotion-conditioned acoustic modeling with controllable in-
tensity for Sinhala and Tamil, classifying six emotion cate-
gories with XLM-RoBERTa at 87% accuracy for Sinhala and
85.43% for Tamil, making it a distinctive contribution for low-
resource assistive technology.
V. CONCLUSION
This research presented a voice-agentic smart reader for
visually impaired users in Sinhala and Tamil that unifies
four components: Tamil article understanding with hybrid
retrieval-based question answering, Sinhala non-linear voice
navigation and intent classification, emotion-aware text-to-
speech, and Intelligent V oice-Guided Navigation (IVGN) for
non-visual document capture. Results across modules show
that structured retrieval control improves Tamil QA reliability,
supervised intent modeling enables accurate Sinhala voice in-
teraction, emotion-aware synthesis improves listening quality,
and geometry-aware guidance supports reliable OCR-ready
capture.
Taken together, these findings show that accessibility-
focused smart reading systems for low-resource languages
benefit from modular integration, explicit control mecha-
nisms, and human-centered feedback rather than model scale
alone. Future work will prioritize long-term user deployment,
citation-grounded QA, broader emotional speech data, and
tighter orchestration across capture, retrieval, navigation, and
speech synthesis.
